Multi-Modal Sensory Deprivation Study

A computational investigation of how convolutional neural networks adapt to different types of sensory deprivation, exploring cross-modal learning and neural adaptation mechanisms.

Research Overview

This study investigates the effects of multi-modal sensory deprivation on the learning capabilities and performance of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). We extend previous work on vertical edge deprivation to explore three distinct deprivation types: visual (vertical edge masking), spatial (noise addition), and temporal (Gaussian blurring).

Key Finding

CNNs exhibit robustness to individual sensory impairments but show cumulative effects under multi-modal deprivation, suggesting interactions between deprivation types.

Research Questions

  • How do different types of sensory deprivation affect CNN performance?
  • What are the cumulative effects of multi-modal deprivation?
  • How do networks adapt to compromised sensory inputs?
  • What insights does this yield for AI robustness and computational neuroscience?

Synergistic Effect

Methodology

Dataset & Architecture

MNIST (70,000 images). MultiModalCNN with two conv layers, fusion layers, and classification layers. AdamW, LR schedule, early stopping.

Deprivation Framework

  • Visual: Sobel-x edge detection + pixel masking (threshold 0.25)
  • Spatial: Gaussian noise (σ = 0.3)
  • Temporal: 5×5 Gaussian blur (σ = 1.0)

Experimental Design

Five matched conditions, identical hyperparams. Up to 12 epochs with early stopping on validation.
