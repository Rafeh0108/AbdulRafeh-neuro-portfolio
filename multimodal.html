Multi-Modal Sensory Deprivation Study

A computational investigation of how convolutional neural networks adapt to different types of sensory deprivation, exploring cross-modal learning and neural adaptation mechanisms.

Research Overview

This study investigates the effects of multi-modal sensory deprivation on the learning capabilities and performance of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). We extend previous work on vertical edge deprivation to explore three distinct deprivation types: visual (vertical edge masking), spatial (noise addition), and temporal (Gaussian blurring).

Key Finding

CNNs exhibit remarkable robustness to individual sensory impairments but show cumulative effects under multi-modal deprivation, suggesting complex interactions between different deprivation types.

Research Questions:

  • How do different types of sensory deprivation affect CNN performance?
  • What are the cumulative effects of multi-modal deprivation?
  • How do neural networks adapt to compromised sensory inputs?
  • What insights can this provide for AI robustness and computational neuroscience?

Synergistic Effect Discovered

The multi-modal deprived model showed a 0.95% accuracy drop, which exceeds the sum of individual deprivation effects (0.65%). This suggests that deprivation types interact synergistically, creating cumulative effects that exceed simple additive models.

Methodology

Dataset & Architecture

We used the MNIST handwritten digit dataset (70,000 images) and designed a MultiModalCNN architecture with two convolutional layers, fusion layers, and classification layers. The model was trained using AdamW optimizer with learning rate scheduling and early stopping.

Deprivation Framework

Three Deprivation Types:

  • Visual: Sobel-x kernel edge detection with pixel masking (threshold: 0.25)
  • Spatial: Gaussian noise addition (σ = 0.3)
  • Temporal: 5×5 Gaussian blur kernel (σ = 1.0)

Experimental Design

Five experimental conditions were established with identical hyperparameters and training procedures. Each model was trained for up to 12 epochs with early stopping based on validation performance.

Research Impact

This work contributes to our understanding of how artificial neural systems adapt to sensory challenges and provides a computational model for studying neural plasticity and compensatory learning mechanisms. The findings have implications for:

  • AI Robustness: Designing systems that operate under suboptimal conditions
  • Computational Neuroscience: Understanding neural adaptation mechanisms
  • Accessibility: Informing assistive technology design
  • Robotics: Systems operating in degraded sensory environments
